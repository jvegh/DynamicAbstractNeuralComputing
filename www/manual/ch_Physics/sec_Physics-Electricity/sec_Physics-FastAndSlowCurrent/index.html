§ 1.4.3 ”Fast” and ”slow” currents

Basically, we can subdivide ion’s speed in a volume into three classes. (We emphasize, again, that this is an abstraction based on some approximations about the interaction between the charge carrier and its environment). The simplest case when the charge carrier is far enough from other change carriers, and we can assume that zero electric field acts on the carrier. In this case, other (non-electric) forces will act on its other attributes (such as mass); from our point of view, it will move as Laws of thermodynamics dictate it. Macrocopically, it will have a drift speed; microscopically, a thermal speed distribution. For that case, we use the word that it moves with its drift speed. This case belongs to thermodynamics. The other simple extreme case when some some external voltage source or internally by some electric or non-electric effect generates a very strong electric field , and the other forces can be neglected apart the electric force. In that case the charge carrier accelerates and decelerates; the independent electric field dictates its motion. In that case, it moves with a potential-accelerated speed. We call it ”fast” current. This ”net” case belongs to electricity and – in a good approximation – can be described by its ideal laws.

The most complex case is when more than one dominant forces remain on the scene and neither of them can be omitted apart the another one(s). This case needs careful analyzis and usually considering Laws of more than one science field. In the case in the focus of our study, the interaction speeds of the two interactions differ by orders of magnitudes, while the gradients are nearly equal. To complicate the business, the two attributes of the moved objects, the charge and the mass, are inseparably connected: changing one changes the other at the same time. The proportion of the two acting forces changes continuously, and so does the microscopic speed of the charge carriers and the macroscopic current’s speed they represent. The common feature of this category that it is not a ”net” case; and that the value of the macroscopic current is about the geometric mean of the of the speeds in the other two mentioned cases. It is definitely a current with a well-defined macroscopic direction, but it cannot be described by the formulas known from the electricity of metals; that is why we call it ”slow current”.
