§ 2.4.3 Voltage and electric field

It is worth to recall some features of voltage. Classical electricity applies a hybrid approach, again. In classical circuits, the voltage arrives instantly, but in the discrete elements, such as condensers and solenoids, have a mathematically described time course. We must extend the notion of electric field by considering the contribution of pseudo-electric field arising from thermodynamics.

For the biological interpretation, we can start from the picture used in the case of condensers: charges arrive into a discrete element and they change a macroscopic feature (called capacity) of that discrete object of the electric circuit. We assume that a driving force exists across the two ends of the condenser. In physical electricity, the ideal condenser is point-like. Given that it is sizeless, the derivative d⁢Ud⁢t cannot be interpreted. In that abstraction, the electric field is a step-like function, see for example Fig. 2.10.

In biology, the objects are extended and the charge carriers are slow ions, so we can ask how the voltage gradient d⁢Ud⁢t changes across biological objects. Here, again, comes into the picture the different charge propagation mechanism. As we discuss in section 2.3.2, the electric and thermodynamic interaction speeds are very different, and the ions have the attributes mass and charge simultaneously, the potential of the charge can travel only with the speed of the slow ions, so the transport equations such as Eq.(2.6) have a wrong theoretical foundation; consequently they need half-empirical coefficients.

Electricity explains that voltage can be measured without or with electric current. Accumulating charge on an insulator results in producing an electrostatic field. Of course, while charging, a current flows and energy is needed to produce a voltage, but it is a one-time action; the created potential difference persists. In a condenser, a counterforce counterbalances the attraction between opposite charges on the opposite plates, no charge movement takes place, and no energy is needed to keep up the voltage. Another possibility is when the charge moves (either an electron of the ’free electron cloud’ moves against the friction of a solid or an ion moves with the Stokes-Einstein speed against viscosity) in a medium. In these latter cases, a current (charge transfer) and potential difference can be observed across the considered space region (a solid conductor or an ion channel). The important difference between producing a voltage difference by charge-up and flowing current is that charge-up is a one-time action, while keeping up a voltage difference while generating the current requieres continuous energy investment.
