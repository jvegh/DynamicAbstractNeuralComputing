§ 2.5.5 Two segments copy

Dipole layers in segment

The electrolyte segment contains dipole molecules, and under the effect of the local electric field (which is represented by the neighboring dipoles), the molecules dissociate and the positive and negative ions leave their counterpart behind and they may cause local macroscopic changes in the electrostatic properties of the electrolyte in the segment. The changes in the local electrical gradient may also change the degree of dissociation and it may produce a graded local electric field. (Given that the concentration also changes, the two gradients may set a balanced state despite that the two gradients are not zero.)

Above, we assumed that in the physical layer the charge carriers are confined in a limited region (in a charged sheet). However, the rest of the cell is filled by electrolyte instead of insulator. We can refine our model in a way that we assume that the electric field is compensated only partially by the charge of the opposite side (i.e., the Gaussian surface contains different amount of charge), due to the different distances to the charged sheets. Due to the planar symmetry of the system, we can assume that the charges in the volume generate an electric field E only in the direction perpendicular to the plane of the membrane, so we deal only with its potential contribution in this direction. We assume that the size of the segment is ”infinite” (compared to the membrane layer’s atomic thickness) in the direction perpendicular to the membrane’s plane. Any charge at distance z experiences a potential from charge sheets with distance d. We assume that the electric fields of the two sheets are constants, and the distance of a charge on axis z differs by the membrane’s thickness, so the two contributing potentials are proportional to the reciprocal’s of the distance to the respective sheets. (We assume that the concentration remains the same and the dissociation rate changes with the distance from the membrane, or more precisely, with the electric field.) We put together the ”infinite” volume with volume charge density σV from ’sheets of charge’ of thickness d⁢z, so the sheet’s surface charge density is c⁢s⁢i⁢g⁢m⁢aV∗d⁢z. Actually, we discuss a one-dimensional distribution.

The contributing potential on the axis at point z of the infinitesimal volume due to the charged sheet

and the total potential due to all elements d⁢z from the left side is (in the mathematical formulas below we express the distance in units of zd, so here z is dimensionless)

Ul⁢e⁢f⁢t=σA2∗ϵo⁢d⁢∫−∞01z⁢𝑑z=σA2∗ϵo∗d⁢[ln⁡|z|]0∞ (2.41)

In the case of a single-segment volume, within the segment, a similar potential with opposite sign, generated by the charges on the opposite side of the considered charged sheet, counterbalance the potential described by Eq.(2.41). However, when a membrane with thickness d separates the segments, the right side of the potential will be

Ur⁢i⁢g⁢h⁢t=σA2∗ϵo⁢d⁢∫0+∞1z+1⁢𝑑z=σA2∗ϵo∗d⁢[ln⁡|z+1|]0∞ (2.42)

The gap increases the potential difference and a potential

U⁢(z)=σA2∗ϵo∗d⁢([ln⁡|z|]−∞0−[ln⁡|z+1|]0∞) (2.43)

awakes in the electrolyte, outside the separated surfaces.

We use the approximation that ln⁡(∞)≈ln⁡(1+∞) and we arrive at

U⁢(z)=σA2∗ϵo∗d∗l⁢n⁢(zz+1) (2.44)

describes the potential along our line outside the ”infinite sheets”. Correspondingly, the electric field is

E⁢(z)=d⁢Ud⁢z=σA2∗ϵo∗(dz∗(z+d)) (2.45)

We assumed that the function can be interpreted for regions (−∞,0) and (1,+∞). However, here were we arrived at the boundary of quantized electricity and continuous electricity. The thickness of the layers is limited at least by the ion’s size. Furthermore, the membrane’s surface is not flat; there are structures (mainly lipids) with size of up to 1⁢n⁢m, so it is probably realistic to consider a layer thickness of 0.5⁢n⁢m (on our mathematical scale 0.1=0.5⁢n⁢m5⁢n⁢m). A similar calculation using layer thickness 0.1.25⁢n⁢m (on our mathematical scale, 0.025) the multiplier is 3. Correspondingly, we assume an electric field

El⁢a⁢y⁢e⁢r=σA2∗ϵo∗[(1z∗z+1)]0.0251=σA2∗ϵo∗3=8.13∗103⁢[Vm∗m⁢M] (2.46)

That is, we can take that by using physically realistic layer thickness the average electric field is two-to-four times more than the value predicted by the ”charged sheet” model. In the followings we use a multiplier 3, (i.e., we consider a layer with thickness 0.125⁢n⁢m proximate to the membrane with a constant electric field E⁢(0.125) (and the same fields between the neuronal plates), and the field in the bulk is described by Eq.(2.45).

The electric field at 0.5⁢n⁢m distance from the membrane is about 5.6∗104⁢V/m, while between the membrane’s surfaces is 1.8∗107⁢m/s; providing nearly three orders of magnitude difference in the value of the electric fields, explaining the reason for introducing potential-assisted and potential-accelerated speeds in Fig. 2.4.

With this electric field, the voltage difference across the membrane at 120⁢m⁢M concentration and 5⁢n⁢m membrane thickness becomes

Ul⁢a⁢y⁢e⁢r=3∗σA2∗ϵo∗l⁢n⁢(0.11.1)∗d=3∗(1.20∗102)∗(2.34∗104)∗(5∗10−9)=23.9⁢m⁢V (2.47)

The logaritmic term describing the membrane’s geometry is the same for any ion, furthermore, the potential’s magnitude is independent of the ion. The dissociation process results in the same concentration of the positive and negative ions on either side, and the condenser’s potential. At a given concentration, the potential difference is caused by the finite width of the membrane plus the dissociation of the ions. Given that the same number of charged ions must be present on the two sides of the membrane, the surface density σA must be the same on the two sides. A higher concentration on one side increases the charge density on one side that increases the electric field that increases dissociation rate on the other side Notice that the effect is purely electrostatic, and will result in an asymmetric distribution of ions; no permeability is needed. If the membrane is permeable, ions will move across the membrane until equilibrium reached. The resulting potential difference depends linearly on the concentration difference.

Figure 2.4 shows the membrane’s extra potential gradient in function of the distance from the membrane’s surface for three different bulk potentials (i.e., different concentrations). Here, we use physical length units (instead of the abstract distances used during the derivation) and an arbitrary voltage gradient scale.

Suppose we assume the estimation given in [1] that in the case of resting potential, the scale of the gradient that accelerates the ions across the ion channel is calibrated approximately as k⁢V/c⁢m. Recall that we are still speaking about the resting state and only about the extra gradient evoked by the finite-width membrane. We are at the boundaries of the macroscopic and microscopic worlds. We derived our integrand from the picture of discrete charges but integrated it into the picture of continuous charge distribution, so we have an empirical factor between them. We assume an atomic layer (a skin) on the surface. However, the layer itself can also be modeled as having just a few ions under their mutual repulsion on the surface or a few atomic layers on top of each other, depending on the concentration and voltage in the bulk on the two sides. (The diagram line is valid in the plane crossing the membrane and the ion channel.)

We assumed that the membrane’s width is 5⁢n⁢m. An ion channel is depicted in the middle of the figure with a diameter of about 1.5⁢n⁢m. Furthermore, we assume that the ion’s size and, correspondingly, the thickness of the atomic layer in the electrolyte on the surface of the membrane is about 0.1⁢n⁢m. For comparison, recall that the size of the tip of the clamp pipette is in the range of 1,000⁢n⁢m and the size of the soma in the range of 10,000⁢n⁢m.

The figure shows three different bulk concentrations, so one can estimate (using non-matching diagram lines) what happens if the concentrations change between the two segments (although their interaction slightly complicates the process). The bulk concentration naturally changes the potential, so a difference in the potentials can be measured. However, this may be the voltage between the bulks, one bulk and one layer, or two layers. When measuring potential in the segments using such a pipette and touching the membrane one actually may measure some average potential which contains only a tiny proportion from the layer, so the mentioned extra potential gradient cannot be measured by that method. Anyhow, the ion currents flow between the two layers under the potential, which can drastically differ from the bulk potential. Derived values, such as the GHK potential, should be rethought.

Our results align with the observation (see caption of 11.22 in [1]: ”A small flow of ions carries sufficient charge to cause a large change in the membrane potential. The ions that give rise to the membrane potential lie in a thin (<1⁢n⁢m) surface layer close to the membrane”. See the dotted line in Fig. 2.5; notice that the x-scale on the figure spans 1⁢n⁢m only. The amount of unbalanced ions is in the range of 107, and so is the amount of rush-in ions. In addition, those ions on the high-concentration side side rush-in to the low-concentration side and cause the large change in the membrane potential. Their absolute amount is small compared to the total number of ions in the cell, but it is significant compared to the number of unbalanced ions.
