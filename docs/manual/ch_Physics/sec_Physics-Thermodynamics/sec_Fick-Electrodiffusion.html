2.3.6 Fick’s Law

By expressing the speed through the Stokes-Einstein relation, see Eq.(2.32)

d⁢Vd⁢t=D∗R∗R∗Tq∗F∗F∗d2⁢Cd⁢x2=(T∗R2q∗F2)∗D∗d2⁢Cd⁢x2 (2.17)

Or, alternatively,

d⁢Vd⁢t=(T∗RF)2⁢k6∗q∗π∗η∗a∗d2⁢Cd⁢x2 (2.18)

Given that

d⁢Vd⁢t=D∗d2⁢Cd⁢x2 (2.19)

expresses Fick’s Second Law of Diffusion, we can derive the ratio between the electric and thermodynamic temporal gradients. Using values T=300⁢K, q=1, F= 96495⁢A∗s/m⁢o⁢l, R=8.31446261815324⁢J∗K−1∗m⁢o⁢l−1

d⁢Vd⁢t=2.23∗10−6∗D∗d2⁢Cd⁢x2=2.23∗𝟏𝟎−𝟔⁢d⁢Cd⁢t (2.20)

We can provide a rough, experimental estimation of the value. The time course of the gradient (half-width) in Figure 2.2 in [6] was produced in 0.3∗103⁢s. Reciprocally, a voltage gradient needs 0.1∗10−3⁢s to produce the same concentration gradient, see Fig. 2d in[71]. Their ratio is 3∗10−6. The other way round, when we assume that the propagation of the electric field in the solution is 2∗108⁢m/s and multiply it by the factor above, we arrive at the speed of ion’s speed in the range around 102⁢m/s.
