§ 2.6.1 Ion channels

As Fig. 1.1 depicts, an ion channel is open roughly for 10−2⁢s and the peak amplitude is 1.6∗10−12⁢A, so the maximum charge that can be transferred in a single shot is 1.6∗10−14⁢C⁢b, which assumes 105 ions per shots. Recall that the number of the uncompensated ions is about 5∗107, the several simultanously working ion channels overcompensate the ion balance: despite the forceful external potential, no more ions can enter the membrane (the ’downhill’ gradient reverses). The entered ions cannot leave sufficiently quickly the proximity of the channel’s exit, so their potential prevents the rest of ions from entering the membrane: the ion layer closes the channel. In the rest of the shown period, despite that the external voltage (the square wave) is still present, the potential of the stalled ions (the internal voltage) keeps the channel closed: no new rising edge (voltage gradient) arrives. Given that after 20⁢m⁢s no more new openings happen, the membrane quietly discharges. Again, the external voltage is stable, the internal voltage decreases, so the aggregate current decreases. The membrane’s conductance does not change. However, the resulting potential that directs the current is the sum of the external and internal potentials. As the charge diffuses toward the bulk region, the internal potential decays, and so does the measurable aggregate current.
