2.2.2 Discrete versus continuous

Science experimentally underpinned that the world around us, although for the first look it seems to be continous, comprises small discrete components. Correspondingly, science has a somewhat schizophrenic view. We know that electric charges are quantized (they are integer multiples of some elementary charge), but Colulomb’s Law has no explicit limitations for the magnitude of the charge or for the distance of the charged particles (say the size of the elementary charge) in the law. Similarly, we consider electric current as continous (differentiable) quantity, although we know it is quantized. In some cases one must connect those two different views. The most prominent case is is thermodynamics, where we explicitly consider particles in the phase space and derive macroscopic features using statistical assumptions. The different omissions and approximations explain, why thermodynamics can be directly applied neither to ionic solutions nor to electrons in the free electron cloud in solids. In the case of biology, the case is even more critical: sometimes the distances are just a few times more than the characteristic size of ions and the number of particles if much less than the number enabling to draw statistically reliable conclusions. However, special geometrical relations and physical assumpptions enable us to bridge the particle view and the continuous view.
